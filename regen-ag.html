Regeneration at Commercial Scale

Most regenerative-agriculture stories are demonstration plots or small conservation ranches. This page is about the other end of the spectrum — growers running thousands of acres of cotton, cherries, corn, and wheat who've adopted biological, soil-first management and are willing to put numbers behind it. We looked closely at one of the field's most visible frameworks — Advancing Eco Agriculture (AEA), founded by John Kempf — plus one grower's own lab-tested results.

🌾 The honest framing: almost everything below is self-reported by the farms and companies involved, not independently re-measured. We say exactly where the evidence is solid and where it isn't.

Honest Caveats
No university, extension, or government trial evaluating AEA's program specifically was found — every number on this page traces back to AEA's own podcast or website, not an outside measurement.
Brix and plant-sap analysis are contested as reliable standalone indicators: University of Maryland and Mississippi State Extension both caution that readings are confounded by temperature, time of day, and plant moisture, with no reliably demonstrated correlation to nutrient density in independent testing.
AEA is a for-profit company that sells both consulting and the proprietary biological inputs (Spectrum, Mycogenesis, OP8) its case studies credit — a structural conflict of interest that isn't disclosed on the case-study pages themselves.
This page's methods are not the same as this site's hydrology research — searches turned up no AEA case study tying their program to measured infiltration rate, Ksat, or aquifer recharge. Their drought-resilience claims are framed theoretically (nutritionally-balanced plants supposedly need less water) rather than backed by field infiltration data.
