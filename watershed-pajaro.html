The Pajaro River: A Real Flood, A Real Counterfactual

On the night of March 10, 2023, the Pajaro River's levee failed and flooded the town of Pajaro. The levee's inadequacy had been known and documented since 1955. Everything below is built from real, independently-checkable data — a real USGS gauge, a real watershed boundary, real reporting on the levee's history — presented alongside exactly what it can and cannot tell us yet.

The Levee's Known Problem

The Pajaro River's levees provided just an 8-year level of flood protection — among the lowest of any federal flood-control system in California. The deficiency was first documented after flood damage in 1955. The Army Corps found the project inadequate again in 1963; Congress authorized reconstruction in 1966, and again in 1986; proposed designs repeatedly stalled on funding and land agreements. A $599 million project — led by USACE, the Pajaro Regional Flood Management Agency, and California DWR — is now underway, targeting 100-year and 25-year protection for Watsonville, Pajaro, and the surrounding farmland.

The levee's original 1998 design capacity was 19,000–22,000 cfs — well above the 13,200 cfs observed at Chittenden in 2023. The "8-year protection" figure describes the levee's degraded, effective capacity by 2023 (decades of sedimentation, vegetation, deferred maintenance), not its original design. These are two different numbers and should not be conflated.

Multiple public reports attribute the decades of delay in part to a federal cost-benefit framework that undervalued the agricultural land and lower-income communities the levee was meant to protect. That is documented public-record reporting, not this project's own characterization — but it is the reason this specific flood, and not a more famous one, is this project's anchor case.

An Illustrative Counterfactual — Not a Simulation

It is tempting to ask: "if upstream farmland had better infiltration capacity, would the levee have held?" We can't answer that yet with a calibrated model of this watershed — that requires the same hindcast-validation step described in the caveats below. Applying an improvement of that magnitude as a scenario assumption — not a Pajaro-specific soil finding — to a simplified peak-attenuation estimate suggests the kind of relative reduction in runoff-driven peak flow this site's Rain In The Valley and San Lorenzo models already illustrate for smaller watersheds. We are not publishing a specific cfs number for Pajaro here, because doing so without the gauge-calibration step below would dress up a guess as a finding.

Honest Caveats
This is not a certified engineering flood study. It does not simulate the physical breach at the actual failure location, and it should not be used for design, permitting, or underwriting decisions.
The terrain and watershed boundary are real (USGS WBD + LiDAR-derived elevation). The March 2023 hydrograph is real (USGS gauge 11159000). The storm-total precipitation figure (5–13 in. over parts of Monterey County) is a credible reported range, not yet a station-level time series suitable as a direct model input.
The counterfactual infiltration assumption borrows an independently-measured number from a different California farm (Singing Frogs Farm) as a scenario analogy — it is not a Pajaro Valley soil survey finding.
Before this model can support a real institutional claim (to a funder, an agency, or eventually an insurer), it needs to reproduce the observed 13,200 cfs peak at Chittenden from real storm input and real land-cover data — the hindcast-calibration step described in the project requirements. That has not been done yet.
