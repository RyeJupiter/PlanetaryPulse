The Techniques That Move The Needle

"Regenerative land management" isn't one thing — it's a set of specific interventions with very different levels of evidence behind them. We ran a fact-checked research pass (multi-source search, then adversarial verification of every claim) to find out which practices actually move Ksat and Manning's n, by how much, and where the evidence gets thin. Six findings survived verification; several plausible-sounding claims did not (see below).

Vegetation & soil structure move the needle the most

Converting annual cropland or continuously-grazed pasture to perennials, agroforestry, or grass buffers gave the largest measured infiltration gains of anything studied — average +59.2% over annual cropping across a 89-study meta-analysis. A Missouri field study found agroforestry buffers raised Ksat 16.7× and macroporosity 5.7× over continuously-grazed pasture. No-till alone was a coin flip (non-significant overall, but +55.6% in wetter climates) — structure and cover matter more than tillage alone.

Meta-analysis (89 studies) & Missouri pasture study — PMC6752860, ResearchGate

Earthworks are situational, not universal

Infiltration swales need native soil infiltration above roughly 10 mm/hr to work without an underdrain — squarely at the boundary of this site's "degraded" band. Check dams are sized with simple ponding-drawdown physics (pond depth ÷ infiltration rate = drain time); a real design example drains a 0.6m pond in 37.5 hours at 16 mm/hr. Translation: measure native infiltration before sizing any earthwork — degraded soil needs amendment or underdrains first.

PLOS Water & STEP/Ontario LID guide — journals.plos.org, sustainabletechnologies.ca

Beaver dam analogs help locally — don't oversell them

Beaver dam analogs (leaky dams) increased mean water residence time +230% and shifted a stream reach from "losing" to "gaining" groundwater in a Utah study. But a separate 2-year, 17-structure trial found no statistically significant change in downstream streamflow at 8 weeks or 1 year — the paper's own conclusion: effects are short-term and confined to within ~7m of the stream. Great for riparian soil moisture and drought buffering near the corridor; not a substitute for watershed-scale land-cover change.

Majerova et al. 2015 (HESS) & Bosworth et al. 2025 (JAWRA) — hess.copernicus.org, sagelandcollaborative.org

Real-world illustration, not a peer-reviewed measurement: Knepp Estate in West Sussex, UK (subject of Isabella Tree's book Wilding) de-canalized the River Adur and reconnected it to its floodplain starting in 2012, then added an enclosed beaver reintroduction in 2020. Knepp and press coverage credit the combination with reduced downstream flooding — but unlike the Utah studies above, that specific claim hasn't gone through this page's adversarial verification process, so treat it as a documented case, not a confirmed number.

Surface Ksat can lie about the subsurface

One watershed study measured Ksat from 0–387 mm/hr, always highest at the surface — but found near-zero subsurface Ksat at both upper-slope and toeslope positions even where the surface exceeded 100 mm/hr. A hardpan or lithologic discontinuity a foot down can throttle aquifer recharge no matter how spongy the topsoil looks. Surface infiltration tests alone can't confirm deep percolation — profile-scale data matters.

Nature Scientific Reports — nature.com

This model's Manning's n range is now cited

USGS and FHWA reference tables (the standard hydraulic-engineering source) give Manning's n from ≈0.02–0.04 for bare/cultivated ground up to 0.07–0.20 for dense shrub/willow/heavy timber where flow reaches the branches. That's the empirical backing for this page's n = 0.012 (concrete) to n = 0.08 (regenerative) range, with room for finer intermediate steps as land recovers from bare soil → grass → shrub → riparian forest.

USGS SIR 2006-5108 & FHWA/USGS WSP 2339 — pubs.usgs.gov, fhwa.dot.gov

The degraded baseline is real, not a strawman

A documented keyline-design restoration case (C-B Ranch, northern New Mexico) measured up to 80% of rainfall running off a mere 3% slope on degraded pasture before treatment. That's this page's "Degraded Farmland" scenario, measured on real ground — the problem regenerative earthworks are meant to correct is not hypothetical.

US Fish & Wildlife Service project page — fws.gov
Checked and did not hold up — plausible-sounding claims that failed adversarial verification during this research pass. Noted here so they don't quietly get reintroduced later:
  • That grazing intensity has a clear negative relationship with infiltration (the source data didn't back this as stated).
  • That check-dam/swale systems retain roughly double their nominal design volume via an "infiltration credit."
  • Specific beaver-dam-analog groundwater-rise figures of 18–30cm extending 135m upstream (the real, sourced figure is smaller and more local: ~12–14cm within 7m).
  • That cover-cropping benefits compound to +30% or more after 4+ years of use.
  • The specific mechanistic claim that keyline design "works by using natural ridges and contours to slow, spread, and sink water" (the ranch-scale runoff data held up; this specific mechanism claim did not).

Open questions this research couldn't answer:

  • Realistic capital/labor cost per acre or per structure for swales, keyline plowing, check dams, BDAs, and riparian buffers — and the install sequence with the fastest payoff.
  • Whether any of this evidence comes from a Mediterranean climate (winter-wet/summer-dry) watershed like the Santa Cruz Mountains, rather than semi-arid Utah, sub-Saharan Africa, or the Midwest US.
  • Actual verified hydrological measurements from White Oak Pastures, GA — named in our original research question, but no quantitative claims about it survived fact-checking. Treat any numbers you see elsewhere about it as unverified.
  • At what scale (number of structures, % of watershed treated) point interventions like BDAs and check dams stop being "short-term and local" and start moving watershed-level streamflow.
