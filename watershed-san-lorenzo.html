⛰ Local Geologic History ▾

The San Lorenzo rises at Castle Rock State Park and runs about 29 miles to Monterey Bay — the largest watershed lying entirely within Santa Cruz County.

The Santa Cruz Mountains themselves are young: uplifted 3–4 million years ago as the Pacific Plate ground against the North American Plate along the San Andreas Fault system, which cuts right through this watershed. That same fault zone caused the 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake (M6.9) — the uplift is still active.

Uplift exposed older marine sedimentary rock to erosion, landslides, and — where fault movement juxtaposed different rock bodies — karst limestone (the caves at Henry Cowell and Bonny Doon) alongside pockets of granitic and metamorphic basement rock.

One formation matters most for water: the Santa Margarita Sandstone, which weathers into the sandy "Zayante soils" of the Santa Cruz Sandhills. That sand drains fast and stores groundwater — the Santa Margarita basin beneath it supplies up to half of the river's dry-season flow. It's the same soil-sponge principle this site argues for, just built by 15 million years of marine deposition instead of a season of cover cropping.
