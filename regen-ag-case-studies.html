Case Studies at Scale

These range from thousand-acre row-crop operations to a handful of much smaller, intensively managed farms — all real, named, commercial operations selling into the market, not demonstration plots. Most of the numbers below are company- or farm-reported; where independent, third-party measurement exists, we've flagged it explicitly.

Carzalia Valley Produce — Columbus, NM

~3,000–3,100 acres · Pima cotton, onions, chile

James Johnson reported 1,490 lbs/acre cotton yield in 2020 against an 880 lbs/acre county average.

Omeg Family Orchards — The Dalles, OR

~3,000–3,600 acres · Cherries

Mike Omeg's operation reported an 89% increase in trunk cross-sectional area versus a control group — the most formally-structured trial in AEA's case-study set.

Singing Frogs Farm — Sebastopol, CA

8 acres (~3 cultivated) · No-till vegetables, since 2007

Elizabeth & Paul Kaiser run permanent no-till beds with continuous high-density multi-cropping (5–7 harvests/bed/year) and zero herbicide, pesticide, or fungicide — fertility comes entirely from compost and mulch. Far smaller than the row-crop farms above, but the best-verified entry on this page.

An independent field study (published in the 2022 book What Your Food Ate) compared Singing Frogs against a neighboring tilled organic farm growing the same crop and measured soil carbon >400% higher, water infiltration ~7× greater (the same Ksat-style metric this site's Hydrology page builds its whole model around), and soil biology ~300% higher — plus produce nutrient density 100–400% above a USDA organic baseline. A separate 10-year UC Davis study measured soil organic matter reaching 1.2% (more conservative than the farm's own 8–10% claim).

Paicines Ranch (Kelly Mulville) — San Benito County, CA

25-acre vineyard · Sheep-integrated viticulture

Vineyard Director Kelly Mulville, applying 25+ years of Savory-style holistic grazing, designed an unusually tall overhead trellis that keeps fruit and leaves above grazing height — letting 1,700+ sheep graze the entire vineyard in a single day, year-round, including during the growing season. That eliminates tillage, mowing, herbicide, suckering, and hand-weeding.

Ranch-reported (not independently audited): soil organic matter up 66% (1.5%→2.5%) from 2014–2021, understory plant species up from 11 to 100+, insect abundance 5× a conventional neighboring vineyard, and 60+ bird species recorded. Most recently featured on the Conservation Stories podcast (Episode 38, March 2025) and earlier on AEA's Regenerative Agriculture Podcast (Episode 80).
