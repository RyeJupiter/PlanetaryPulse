The Physics of the Soil Sponge

Flood outcomes emerge from the physical condition of the land. When a storm drops millions of gallons over a watershed, the response is governed by two properties: how quickly water can enter the ground, and how much resistance the surface offers to water that stays on top of it.

Maximizing infiltration (Kₛₐₜ)

Living soils are laced with root channels, fungal mycelium, and macropore networks that give water somewhere to go. Saturated hydraulic conductivity climbs from an abysmal 0–10 mm/hr on compacted, degraded ground to 50–150 mm/hr under regeneratively managed land. The storm quietly disappears into the ground instead of racing downhill.

Attenuating flow (Manning's n)

Structural roughness — dense root systems, multi-species cover crops, micro-topography — dissipates the kinetic energy of moving water. Higher Manning's n means slower velocities, longer travel times, and a flat runoff curve where a bare landscape would produce a violent flash-flood peak.

The three scenarios below share identical topography and receive the same storm. Only the two soil parameters change — and that is enough to move the outlet hydrograph from catastrophe to non-event.

Honest Caveats
Runoff ratio isn't pure infiltration — evapotranspiration and storage confound it. Track B output is a scenario, not a prediction.
SCS Curve Number is a lumped, empirical parameter. Scaling plot-scale gains to a whole watershed is approximate.
Infiltration benefits are largest for small and medium storms and shrink for extremes as soil saturates. Don't overclaim on the 500-year flood — the real win is frequent floods and dry-season water.
Manning's n and Kₛₐₜ values in Track A are illustrative and drawn from published literature ranges. Site-specific SSURGO measurements will replace them in Track B.
