## The idea worth spreading (one sentence)

Floods, drained aquifers, and a bankrupt federal flood-insurance system are the same problem
wearing three masks — degraded land that can't hold water — and we can fix all three by paying
farmers to rebuild the soil's capacity to drink, with satellites to prove it worked.

## Short abstract (≈100 words — typical application field)

When a river floods, we treat it as a water problem and send a FEMA check. But the flood didn't
start when the river rose — it started when the soil stopped drinking. Decades of degraded farmland
have turned living sponges into hardpan that sheds rain into flash floods instead of recharging
aquifers. The fix is upstream and underfoot: regenerative farming rebuilds the soil's water-holding
capacity. I'll show, using free NASA satellite data, that this change is already measurable from
space — and argue we should redirect disaster dollars to pay farmers to hold water before it floods.

## Why this talk, why now

- **A system is visibly breaking.** The National Flood Insurance Program is roughly $20B in debt to
  the U.S. Treasury and underwater on its own books; billion-dollar flood disasters keep climbing.
  We are paying for floods on the back end.
- **The fix is unglamorous and underfoot.** Each ~1% increase in soil organic matter lets an acre of
  land hold on the order of 20,000+ more gallons of water. Multiply across a watershed and you've
  built flood-control infrastructure out of biology — no concrete required.
- **It's now provable from space.** This is the new part. Satellite records (NASA MODIS, SMAP soil
  moisture, GRACE groundwater) let anyone watch a regenerated landscape green up, cool down, and hold
  more water over 20+ years. Measurement was the missing ingredient that kept this idea anecdotal.
  That's what I've built with EarthPulse.

## Talk arc (5 beats, ~9–12 min)

1. **The check** — open on a flood and the FEMA payout that follows. Establish the cost and the
   reflex: we pay *after*.
2. **The reframe** — the flood started in the soil. Soil as a sponge; degraded land as pavement.
   The water had nowhere to go but sideways and fast.
3. **The mechanism** — rebuild the sponge (cover crops, no-till, managed grazing) → water infiltrates
   → flood peaks drop downstream *and* the aquifer rises. One intervention, two wins, plus the farm
   gets more resilient and the land cools.
4. **The proof** — live EarthPulse moment: a real regeneration site seen from space over two decades —
   greener, cooler, wetter. The signal is real and it's already there. (Al-Baydha, Loess Plateau, or
   a U.S. flood-prone watershed.)
5. **The ask** — stop paying only for floods. Pay farmers to hold water *before* it floods, and use
   satellites to verify it. Redirect the disaster dollar upstream.
