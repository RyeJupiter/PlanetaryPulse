## Main post

The flood didn't start when the river rose. It started when the soil stopped drinking.

I've been building **EarthPulse** — a tool that uses 25 years of NASA satellite data to make
landscape regeneration visible from space.

You can watch it happen: degraded land greening up (vegetation/NDVI) and cooling down (land-surface
temperature) at real regeneration sites around the world — Al-Baydha in Saudi Arabia, the Loess
Plateau in China, White Oak Pastures in Georgia. The "regeneration signature" shows up as a slope
you can measure.

Here's where it's going, and why I can't stop thinking about it:

When we degrade farmland, soil loses its ability to absorb water. Rain that used to soak in and
recharge aquifers now sheets off into flash floods. We pay for those floods on the back end — FEMA,
the National Flood Insurance Program (billions in debt to the Treasury), rising premiums.

But the fix is upstream and underfoot. Regenerative farming rebuilds the soil's capacity to drink.
More water infiltrating = lower flood peaks downstream AND more water banked in the aquifer for
farmers in the dry season. Same rain — redirected from destructive-fast to beneficial-slow.

So I'm building a model: connect rainfall to river flow to measure how much water a watershed
currently sheds vs. absorbs — then show what happens if its farmland adopted the infiltration gains
we already measure on real regenerative farms. The output is a number: fewer floods, more farm
water, and avoided FEMA payouts that could instead pay farmers to hold water upstream.

EarthPulse is the measurement layer that makes that fundable.

I'm prototyping it on the Pajaro River — the 2023 Watsonville levee breach, $300M+ in damage, an
intensely agricultural valley.

If you work in climate, water, ag, or insurance, I'd genuinely love your eyes on it. And if you run
a climate community in SF, I'd love to come present.
