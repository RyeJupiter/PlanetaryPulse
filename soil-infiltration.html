Seeing Infiltration: The Slake Test & Rainfall Simulator

Ksat — the infiltration number this site's model runs on — sounds abstract until you watch it happen. Soil-health educators have been demonstrating it in the field for years with two simple, standardized tests. Here's what's actually going on in the photos you've probably seen.

The Slake Test
Photo: Jason Johnson, USDA/NRCS Iowa — public domain

Take two air-dried soil clods — one from tilled ground, one from no-till or cover-cropped ground — and drop them into wire-mesh baskets, submerged in clear jars of water at the same moment.

The tilled clod has weak aggregation: without living roots and fungal networks binding particles together, water rushes into every crack and the whole thing collapses, clouding the jar. The healthy clod holds its shape — its aggregates are strong enough to resist slaking apart, so the water around it stays clear.

This is a direct, visible proxy for aggregate stability — the same soil structure that determines Ksat. Soil that survives the slake test is the same soil that lets a storm disappear into the ground instead of running off.

The Rainfall Simulator
Photo: Lance Cheung, USDA — public domain (USDA "Bundled Benefits of Soil Health" event, 2014)

NRCS's traveling rainfall simulator rains on several soil trays at once — usually tilled cropland, no-till with a cover crop, overgrazed grassland, well-managed grazing land, and sometimes bare pavement — side by side, under identical "rainfall." Clear jars catch what runs off each tray versus what soaks in.

The tilled and overgrazed trays produce fast, muddy runoff — you can watch their jars fill almost immediately. The cover-cropped and well-managed trays barely produce runoff at all; nearly everything infiltrates. It's this site's three-scenario thesis, played out on an actual table.

Honest Caveats
These are field demonstrations, not laboratory Ksat measurements — they show the right relative ordering (degraded < regenerative) vividly, but the exact numbers depend on which soil, which storm, and which day.
Gabe Brown's own infiltration story (0.5 in/hr on his North Dakota cropland in 1991, rising after switching to no-till/cover crops/diverse rotations) gets quoted with different "after" numbers across different talks — some say 8 in/hr, others ~30 in/hr or "1 inch in 9 seconds." Treat these as retold field anecdotes from his talks, not a single fixed, replicated measurement.
Both tests are standardized by NRCS for consistent field use (see the fact sheets above) — they're legitimate, official soil-health-education methodology, just not substitutes for a certified soil lab test.
